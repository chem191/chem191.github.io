CHEM 191: A Teensy Introduction to Atmospheric Chemistry

Syllabus

CHEM 191: Issues in Environmental Chemistry

Chemistry, Earth, and Air: A very short introduction

Instructor: Dr. Al Fischer, PhD
Office Hours: By appointment at calendly.com/drfischer; drop-ins welcome. (NS 209)
Email: dfischer@wcu.edu

Chem 191: Chemistry, Earth, and Air is a freshman seminar course with an emphasis on atmospheric chemistry. The course is geared toward anyone with an interest in chemistry, atmospheric science, environmental science, and/or engineering. Students will learn through a combination of lectures and a semester-long project to construct and deploy Arduino-based atmospheric sensors. An emphasis will be placed on overarching concepts and problem-solving skills rather than rote minutia.

Specific Learning Objectives

  1. Demonstrate the ability to program using the Arduino IDE.
  2. Demonstrate an ability to solder basic electronic components.
  3. Demonstrate an ability to design an electronics enclosure.
  4. Demonstrate an ability to use Excel for basic data analysis.
  5. Demonstrate atmospheric literacy, as outlined below.
  6. Understand how the scientific process works, both in theory and in practice.
  7. Show preparedness for future college classes, especially those in the sciences and engineering.

Themes

These themes are taken verbatim from the Atmospheric Science Literacy Framework and will be used as guideposts throughout the semester.

  • Earth has a thin atmosphere that sustains life.
  • Energy from the Sun drives atmospheric processes.
  • Atmospheric circulation transports matter and energy.
  • Earth’s atmosphere changes over time and space, giving rise to weather and climate.
  • Earth’s atmosphere continuously interacts with the other components of the Earth System.
  • We seek to understand the past, present, and future behavior of Earth’s atmosphere through scientific observation and reasoning.
  • Earth’s atmosphere and humans are inextricably linked.

Prerequisites

Students should be comfortable with algebra and basic statistics (i.e. mean and standard deviation). A familiarity with chemistry and/or experience programming is helpful but not required.

Required Course Materials

The following books are required and are available from the WCU bookstore and most other book sellers. Additional required reading material will be posted on the Instructor’s website and Blackboard.

  • Air Pollution and Global Warming, Mark Z. Jacobsen, Cambridge University Press (rental book)
  • Chemistry: A very short introduction, Peter Atkins, Oxford University Press. (supplemental text, ~$10)
  • The Atmosphere: A very short introduction, Peter Atkins, Oxford University Press. (supplemental text, ~$10)

Additional required materials:
- A scientific calculator (not on a cell phone). An acceptable calculator can be purchased for less than $10. It should have the capability to do exponents and trigonometry.

Technology

Technology requirements

Students will need: - A laptop computer with at least one standard USB-A port or, for computers without a USB-A port (e.g. newer MacBooks), an appropriate adapter. - The Arduino integrated development environment (IDE) and Teensyduino add-on. These software are freely available from the developers for Mac OSX, Windows, and Linux.

In addition, students will be provided with components to construct an air quality sensor, which they must return in working order at the end of the semester. Any student wishing to keep their sensor should purchase their own materials ahead of time through Mouser Electronics (a bill of materials is available by request, total cost is about $100).

Personal technology use policies

The use of cell phones during lecture is prohibitied; the sparing use of cell phones during workshop/lab sessions is permitted but discouraged. If you must, please use your cell phone outside the classroom.

In an effort to maintain a productive work environment for all students, please refrain from playing music, videos, etc., in the classroom.

Off-campus Work

Students will occasionally be required to travel off-campus to setup and retrieve data from their sensors. The round-trip travel distance typically won’t be more than 10 miles, although students will be required to sign a waiver before conducting any work off-campus.

Grading

Conventions: The grades of A+ (100-97), A (96.9-93), A- (92.9-90), B+ (89.9-87), B (86.9-83), B- (82.9-80), C+ (79.9-77), C (76.9-73), C- (72.9-70), D+ (69.9-67), D (66.9-63), D- (62.9-60) and F (< 60) indicate gradations in quality from Excellent to Failure. Please note that a C- grade is less than satisfactory and may not meet particular program and/or course requirements. Students must be familiar with the class attendance, withdrawal, and drop-add policies and procedures. A percentage breakdown of the final grade is given in the table below.

Category Weight
Participation and Attendance 10%
Quizzes & Homework 15%
Lab Assignments 15%
Midterm Exam (take home) 15%
Successful Sensor Build (+ return at end of semester) 20%
Final Exam (technical paper detailing sensor and results from it) 25%

Assignment Submissions: Students will be required to submit assignments electronically and as hard copies throughout the semester. Each assignment will state how it should be turned in.

  • For electronic assignments:
    • Each student will be assigned a personal Dropbox folder to which they will submit assignments.
    • Files should follow the convention firstnameLastname_assignmentName.xyz.
    • Any files not named appropriately, not in the specified format, or submitted elsewhere will either incur point deductions or not be graded.
    • Electronic assignments are due at 23:59:59 on the due date or will be counted late.
  • For hard-copy assignments:
    • Students will turn in a paper copy of the assignment either in class or to the instructors office.
    • Students should complete any worksheets on the worksheet itself and should not reformat the worksheet.
    • Any hard-copy assignments that are turned in electronically will not be graded.
    • Hard-copy assignments are due during class on the due date unless otherwise specified.

Late Work: Quizzes, homework, and lab assignments will be accepted up to 7 days late with a deduction of 5 points per day; no work will be accepted after 7 days (including weekends). Exams will not be accepted late.

Additional Notes: Tests may be graded by computer software. Any answers not in the specified format will not be graded. Written work may be checked for plagiarism using computer software. Plagiarism will NOT be tolerated (specific policies in Fine Print, below).

Course Policies

Classroom Expectations: The shared learning space of the college classroom is built on respect for each other and each other’s learning. Learning together means our actions can intentionally and unintentionally distract others from their learning goals. As responsible learners who respect the rights of others and vow to minimize avoidable distractions such as: non- academic technology use, coming in late, sleeping, off-topic discussions, doing other homework, eating, etc.

Attendance: Attendance to ALL lab periods is mandatory. You must arrive fully prepared and on time (i.e. 5 minutes early) to each lab period. Makeup labs may not be possible. Remember, 10% of your grade is based on attendance and participation. Please email your instructor if you encounter circumstances that absolutely prevent you from making it to lab on time. In the event of inclement weather please check the University website for campus closings; use common sense when attempting to get to campus and notify your instructor if you are unable to make it. Announcements will be made via e-mail if class must be cancelled when the University has not officially closed.

Composition-Condition Marks: Proper mastery of the English language will be considered when assessing lab reports. A student whose written work fails to meet acceptable standards will be assigned a composition-condition (CC) mark by the instructor on the final grade report. All undergraduates who receive two CC grades prior to the semester in which they complete 110 hours at WCU are so notified by the registrar and are required to pass English 300 or English 401 before they will be eligible for graduation. This course must be taken within two semesters of receiving the second CC and must be passed with a grade of C (2.0) or better. Students needing assistance with writing are encouraged to get help from WaLC. All written work submitted for this class may be checked with SafeAssign.

Civil Discourse at Western Carolina: Consistent with WCU’s core values and our campus creed, the WCU community accepts the freedoms and responsibilities of our shared community. WCU encourages all to clearly express their own views while at the same time seeking to understand the varieties of style, identity, and opinion that are held in any diverse community. In order for us to sustain a learning environment that promotes and values freedom of expression, we have a shared charge to accept personal responsibility for our actions, reactions, and speech, while seeking to learn from the actions, reactions, and speech of others.
